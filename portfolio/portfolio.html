Mechatronics Lab for Graduate Students

For my graduate mechatronics course, small teams were asked to take a simple principle of mechatronics and develop a short (6-8 hour) lab based on that principle for future classes. My team was tasked with using strain gauges and instrument amplification. We elected to develop a lab to demonstrate how a simple instrument amplification (InAmp) chip and Arduino UNO could be used in junction with strain gauges to study and characterize a relatively complex dynamic mechanical system. My role in this project was group leader, primarily for document and timeline organization, as well as designing and implementing the physical system, showing that the analytical approximation correlates well with this system.

Figure 1: Schematic of electromechanical components.

I designed a vibrating cantilever beam system, excited by a stepper motor and eccentric mass at the tip of the beam. A diagram of the system is shown in Figure 1. Special considerations during this process included selected a beam geometry which had a natural frequency safely below the Nyquist frequency of the UNO. In addition, the peak amplitude had to be large enough to be detected by the InAmp and UNO circuit, but not so large as to cause plastic deformation at the base, which would have damaged the strain gauges. Figure 2 shows the final experimental system.

Figure 2: Overview of final experimental system. The black box holds all the electronics, with the exception of the motor shield, which needed to be unplugged for certain tests.

After verifying our experimental and analytical systems, my team and I drafted a comprehensive lab pack (which can be downloaded here). I was primarily focused on the lab instructions, while my teammates created a solution manual and organized our presentation.

Figure 3: Sample experimental data during pluck test, plotted over time in Excel. Clipping occurs due to the UNO being unable to detect negative voltages.

Oil Platform Blowout Preventer Failure

Due to the confidential nature of this project, certain details have been omitted.

In the spring of 2011, an oil well began flowing at the surface, what is referred to as a blowout. All oil rigs are equipped with failsafe hydraulic shutoff values, known as a blowout preventer (BOP). However, when the rig BOPs were activated, the well continued to flow at the surface, causing the rig and well to be lost in an explosion (Figure 1). The resulting damages were claimed for over $5M in losses.

Figure 1: Photographs of the extracted failed safety components.

During the evidence tear-down and document review process, my group determined that certain critical components were not OEM parts, but rather less expensive third-party components. After many years of discussion between the insurance, operating, and well owning companies, there was no consensus as to whether the BOP failure was due to manufacturing differences between these components, known as packers, or if it was operator error. My team was again retained for an analytical comparative study of the OEM and third-party packers.

Figure 2: CAD model of dissimilar packer plate geometries in Abaqus CAE.

During this process, my role was inspecting, dimensioning, and modeling the two packer geometries, as well as simulating the BOP activation process with each to determine if the geometric differences had a significant impact in the sealing progressing of the well. The models were a combination of 3D hyperelastic elastomer elements, non-deformable skeletal support geometry, and analytical-rigid surfaces (Figure 2). The results of my simulations showed that the sealing pattern was significantly different and, when compared with the erosion patterns within the subject BOP, were the most likely cause of the well failure. The differences in sealing patterns are shown in Video 1, which has been censored for confidentiality. The video will play the sealing sequence twice, the first to show the progression, the second to emphasize the comparison with the physical evidence extracted from the well.

Video 1: Time progression of dissimilar packers closing on pipe. Surfaces not in contact are shown as red, while sealed surfaces are shown as green.

While this case did go to trial, a settlement was made before the evidence went to deliberation. This outcome was attributed primarily to the analytical analysis performed by myself and my team.

Brass Fittings in Potable Water Systems

Due to the confidential nature of this project, certain details have been omitted.

Over the course of several years, various consumer complaints were made that a particular brand of brass fitting was resulting in property damage due to premature corrosion and subsequent plumbing leakage. Due to the scale of potential damages, a class action lawsuit was filed against the manufacturer. My team was retained to evaluate these fittings and determine the underlying cause of any premature failure.

Figure 1: Example of extracted evidence, which includes fittings and tubing.

My role in this investigation was primarily focused around extracted evidence (an example shown in Figure 1). I photographed, categorized, and inventoried thousands of samples, developed test protocols, and conducted inter-party inspections over the course of a year. The tests varied in complexity, ranging from a simple microscopy to accelerated environmental exposure.

Figure 2: Exemplary sample that had been sectioned, mounted, polished, and imaged under a microscope to study the corrosion of the brass.

Most notable was a three-day leak-testing inspection involving three separate parties, in which approximately 200 extracted samples were photographed and leak tested at our facilities. I oversaw the testing and sample organization, as well maintaining notes and records of the 10 lawyers and experts present as witnesses of the inspection. In addition to leak testing, I also developed and conducted the test protocol for additional sample tear-down, mounting, polishing, and microscopic metallurgical examination (shown in Figure 2).

Electrical Transmission Arm Failures

Due to the confidential nature of this project, certain details have been omitted.

In the fall of 2013, multiple high-power electrical transmission tower arms began showing signs of cracking at the weld between the arm and mounting bracket (Figure 1). In some cases, the arm completely fractured from the bracket, leading to hundreds of miles of transmission arms being taken out of service for inspection. Our client retained my group for a comprehensive analysis of the failure, based on wind and arm strain data collected before the arms were removed from service. My role was to review the provided manufacturing and installation documentation, inspect the subject arm brackets, and develop a versatile finite element model to explore our failure mode hypotheses.

Figure 1: Example of failed bracket arm, showing arm separated from bracket at the weld.

Based on the manufacturing design specifications, I modelled the arm and bracket in Solidworks and imported the geometry to Abaqus CAE. After meshing and boundary conditions, I used the strain data from the previous wind studies to validate my model. My initial results, using a simple static model, suggested that service loads alone could not have caused the premature weld fractures. After a physical examination of multiple subject failed arms, I determined that the spacing between the mounting plates of the bracket was regularly over-tolerance. In addition, I reviewed the field installation notes, which showed inconsistent bolt tightening sequences and torques. To estimate the effects of these pre-service defects, I expanded the static model to include full-contact bolting of the arm to the pole, which I manipulated to consider various arm manufacturing and installation scenarios.

Figure 2: Estimated stress contours resulting from bracket design and installation errors.

My results, shown in Figure 2, demonstrated that with a gap at the maximum design tolerance, smaller than the gap recorded during the inspection, and a correct bolt tightening sequence and torque, the stress in the weld would surpass yield in static conditions. Accounting for the additional effects of cycling dynamic wind loads, I demonstrated that the arm design tolerances would cause the welds to fail by yield and fatigue well before their expected service life. Based on my findings and our full report, our client was able to begin taking corrective action and consider liabilities.

Record Player Restoration

After finding my partner's grandparent's turntable, I was inspired to restore it to it's original working condition. After some preliminary research, I determined it was a 1956 Admiral Classic turntable. Original High Fidelity. Up until now, I had assumed I would gut the internal electronics and replace them with the functional components of a more modern turntable. However, the internals were overall in superb condition. After seeing this, I decided to keep everything as original as possible. I ordered some spare needles and tubes from eBay and began looking into the state of the electronics.

Figure 1: Record amp internals. All original capacitors were cardboard with wax.

I had hoped to reference the component sizes and ratings from the casings, but underestimated the quality of 60-year-old electronics. I thankfully found a circuit schematic to reference on a radio museum website. I considered designing a PCB to simply replace the birds-nest wiring (shown in Figure 1), but I wanted to preserve the antique sound. After researching radio refurbishment, I decided to replace all of the capacitors, a select number of resistors, and the power cord. I also elected to instll a 5A inline fuse.

Figure 2: After replacing all capacitors and select resistors.

While the record player is now functional (and filling my evenings with Steely Dan), there are a few more items I would like to address to improve quality. The motor speed is irregular, and could easily be fixed by a modern DC motor and PSU. Additionally, the motor-to-table drive mechanism is a thin belt, which has began to harden and elongate. This could likely be replaced with an off-the-shelf replacement.
